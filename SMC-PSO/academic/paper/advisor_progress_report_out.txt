1     System Model: Equations and Parameter Choices


1.1    State Vector and Coordinate Definition
The DIP state is a 6-vector:
                                                               ⊤
                                 x = x, θ1 , θ2 , ẋ, θ̇1 , θ̇2 ∈ R6
                                    
                                                                                                    (1)

where x is cart position, θ1 and θ2 are pendulum link angles (from vertical), and dots denote
time derivatives.

1.2    Equations of Motion
The full Lagrangian model is:

                            M(q) q̈ + C(q, q̇) q̇ + G(q) + Ffric = B u                              (2)

where q = [x, θ1 , θ2 ]⊤ , u ∈ [−150, 150] N is the cart force, and B = [1, 0, 0]⊤ selects the actuated
direction.

Inertia matrix M(q) (3×3, symmetric): The matrix is configuration-dependent and
varies 40–60% across the workspace. Its 3 unique entries (out of 6 due to symmetry) involve all
cross-coupling terms between cart, link 1, and link 2:

                                    M11 = M + m1 + m2
                                    M12 = (m1 lc1 + m2 l1 ) cos θ1
                                    M13 = m2 lc2 cos θ2
                                              2
                                    M22 = m1 lc1 + m2 l12 + I1
                                    M23 = m2 l1 lc2 cos(θ1 − θ2 )
                                              2
                                    M33 = m2 lc2 + I2


Coriolis/centrifugal C(q, q̇): Includes centrifugal terms (θ̇i2 ) and Coriolis cross-terms (θ̇i θ̇j ).
Also includes gyroscopic effects (enabled in config).

Gravity vector G(q):

                     G2 = −(m1 lc1 + m2 l1 )g sin θ1 ,       G3 = −m2 lc2 g sin θ2                  (3)

Friction model: Asymmetric viscous + Coulomb friction for all joints:

                                     Ffric = bc ẋ + b1 θ̇1 + b2 θ̇2                                (4)

with 10% variation in friction coefficients used in Monte Carlo studies (Section 5).

2     Sliding Mode Control: Surface Design and Control Laws


2.1    Sliding Surface
The multi-variable sliding surface for the DIP is:
                                  σ = λ1 θ1 + λ2 θ2 + k1 θ̇1 + k2 θ̇2                         (5)
Equivalently, in error coordinates ei = θi − θi∗ (where θ∗ = 0 at upright equilibrium):
                                  σ = λ1 e1 + λ2 e2 + k1 ė1 + k2 ė2                         (6)

The surface (5) is a hyperplane in R4 (angle + rate space). On this surface, the reduced
dynamics are first-order linear and stable by design (eigenvalues at −λi /ki ).

2.2.2      Super-Twisting Algorithm (STA-SMC)
The STA operates on the surface derivative, not the surface itself:
                                     u = u1 + u2                                                        (8)
                                                    α
                                    u1 = −K1 |σ| sign(σ)        (continuous)
                                              Z t
                                    u2 = −K2      sign(σ(τ )) dτ     (integral)

with K1 > K2 > 0 and standard exponent α = 0.5.
Key property: u1 is continuous (chattering reduction), while u2 handles steady-state errors
via integration. Near σ = 0, regularization replaces |σ|α with δ α (where δ = 10−10 ) to avoid
numerical singularity.
6 gains: [c1 , λ1 , c2 , λ2 , K1 , K2 ]; constraint K1 > K2 enforced during PSO.

2.2.3      Adaptive SMC
The switching gain adapts online:
                                                                 σ 
                                               u = −K(t) sat                                            (9)
                                                                   ε
                           K̇(t) = γ|σ| − δleak K(t)          with dead zone |σ| < dz                  (10)
where γ is the adaptation rate, δleak = 0.01 prevents gain windup, and dz = 0.05 suppresses
noise-driven adaptation. K(t) is clamped to [Kmin , Kmax ] = [0.1, 100].
5 gains: [k1 , k2 , λ1 , λ2 , γ].

2.2.4      Hybrid Adaptive STA-SMC
Combines adaptive gain scheduling with the super-twisting structure:
                                                          Z
                       u = −k1 (t) |σ|0.5 sign(σ) − k2 (t) sign(σ) dt                                  (11)

Adaptation laws for both gains k1 (t), k2 (t) use rates γ1 = 0.1, γ2 = 0.05 with a rate limiter of
5.0/s.
Known instability (documented): Under large disturbances and adaptive gain scheduling,
this controller produces 666.9◦ overshoot (176% chattering increase). Root cause: feedback
loop between k1 (t) adaptation and the |σ|0.5 term amplifies errors when |σ| ≫ 1. This is an
open research item.

6     Boundary Layer Study: MT-6 Honest Result


The boundary layer width ε in (7) controls the chattering/accuracy tradeoff.

ε       Chattering (∆ from baseline)           Overshoot             Settling   Notes
0.01                       baseline (high)        nominal         nominal       Too narrow, noisy
0.02                      −3.7% (optimal)         nominal         nominal       Near-optimal
0.05                                −10%           +7.5%         degraded       Too wide, accuracy loss

Important correction: An initial analysis reported 66.5% chattering reduction from adaptive
boundary layer ε(t) = εmin + κ|σ|. A frequency-domain re-analysis showed this was a transient
metric artifact (the initial transient dominated the RMS computation). The unbiased frequency-
domain metric gives only 3.7%.
Conclusion: Fixed ε = 0.02 is near-optimal for the DIP. Adaptive boundary layers are not
justified by this result.

All controllers tested under parametric uncertainty: ±10% and ±20% on all physical parameters
(cart mass, link masses, lengths, inertias). Friction coefficients varied by ±10%.

The Adaptive and Hybrid controllers tolerate larger uncertainty because the adaptive gain
K(t) compensates for the unknown bounds automatically (consistent with the ISS Lyapunov
